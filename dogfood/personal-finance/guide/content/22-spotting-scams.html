Track 3: Putting it together and staying safe

Spotting scams and phishing before they cost you

How to recognize a money scam in the first ten seconds, when the only goal is to make you act before you think.

Picture the idea

A text arrives: "ALERT from your bank. Suspicious charge. Reply with the 6-digit code we just sent to verify, or your account will be locked in 15 minutes." The code is real, because the scammer triggered a genuine login and needs you to read it back so they can finish signing in as you.

Treat it like a smoke alarm with several sensors. One beep might be burnt toast, but heat plus smoke plus a fast alarm means leave the building. Each scam tell is a sensor; you act on the pattern, not on proving any one sensor.

Change the channel and watch the pressure vanish. Instead of replying to the text, open your bank's app yourself. A real alert is still there and waits for you; a scam has no way to follow you to the trusted channel, so the urgency was the tell.

Core ideas

A scam is engineered to bypass thought by manufacturing urgency and borrowing the identity of someone you trust. The content varies, the structure does not: create fear or excitement, demand a fast action, and route the money or access through a path that cannot be reversed. Recognizing the structure beats memorizing this week's particular story.

The defensive move never changes. Stop, do not use the contact details in the message, and reach the institution through a number or app you already trust. A legitimate bank, employer, or tax agency will not ask for your password, a one-time code, a gift card, or a wire under time pressure. If a request fails that test, it is a scam regardless of how official it looks.

A caller says they are from your bank's fraud team and read your real name and last four digits, then ask for the one-time code just texted to you. Why is this still a scam?

  • A real fraud team never needs your one-time code; the caller is using it to finish logging in as you, and knowing your details is cheap for a scammer.
  • It cannot be a scam because they knew your real details.
  • Banks never call about fraud.
  • It is only a scam if they have an accent.

Correct: the secret-request tell is decisive; the one-time code is exactly what the scammer is missing. The tempting wrong answer is "they knew my details," but names and last-four digits leak constantly and prove nothing, which is why the rule keys on the code request, not on what they appear to know.

Where this shows up

You are mid-month, money is tight, and a text says your bank account is locked over a suspicious charge. The fear of losing access right when you are stretched is the lever; that is the moment to open your real banking app instead of tapping the link. The same structure hits as a fake employer email about your direct deposit and a fake delivery-fee text the week a package is due.

The smoke-alarm metaphor breaks for the patient, relationship-style scams: a romance or investment scam builds trust over weeks with no single urgent beep, then asks for money once. There the tell is not urgency but the eventual irreversible payment to someone you have never met in person, so you watch for the payment request rather than the alarm.

Figure it out from scratch

Start With a Real Question

Is this message safe to act on right now?

  • Looking official is not evidence
  • Need a rule that ignores the story

Say It in Plain Words

Count the tells, then verify on a channel you trust.

  • Pattern over any single sign
  • Switch channels before acting

What It Is Made Of

Four tells: urgency, secret request, irreversible payment, mismatch.

  • Each raises risk
  • Two or three together mean stop

State the Rule

Urgency plus a request for a secret or an irreversible payment means scam: stop and verify on a trusted channel.

  • A decision rule, not arithmetic
  • Consistency guard: a real institution still works after you hang up and call back

Try the Simplest Case

"Reply with your code or lose access" scores two tells.

  • Urgency + secret request
  • Rule fires: stop and verify

Find Where It Breaks

Slow trust-building scams show no urgency.

  • Romance and investment cons
  • Watch the irreversible payment instead

Explain It to a Friend

A scam works by rushing you and pretending to be someone you trust. Instead of judging the story, count the tells: urgency, a request for a secret like a code or password, a demand for an irreversible payment, and a sender that almost matches the real one. The rule is simple: urgency plus a secret or irreversible-payment request means stop and verify on a channel you already trust, like your own banking app. It breaks for slow romance and investment scams that build trust before asking once, so there you watch for the payment to a stranger instead of the alarm.

How to reason about this

Check your understanding

  • A message scores only one tell (a mismatched sender, but no urgency or payment ask). What is the safe next step, and why is "ignore the rule because it is just one tell" wrong?
  • Why does switching to a trusted channel defeat almost every version of this, regardless of the story?
  • Name one scam that shows no urgency, and say which tell you rely on instead.

Show you understand

Take the last suspicious message you actually received, score it against the four tells, state whether the rule fires, and write the exact trusted-channel step you would take to verify.

Build it yourself

Create a personal scam-screening card you can apply to any message in under ten seconds.

  1. Gather real inputs: collect three real messages you have received (a bank text, a delivery text, a "prize" or job email).
  2. Apply this module's tool: score each against the four tells, marking which are present.
  3. Predict, then check: decide scam or safe by the rule, then verify each one on a trusted channel and see if your call held.
  4. Sanity-check against a known anchor: any message asking for a one-time code or a gift card should score as a scam every time; if your card lets one through, the card is wrong.

You have it when your card correctly flags every message that requests a code, password, gift card, or wire under pressure, and your verify step always routes through a channel from the message is not in. This is a rubric check against criteria, not runnable code and not a computed number.

Push further: add the slow-scam case to your card, a line for "no urgency but asks for money to someone never met in person," so romance and investment cons are covered too.

Practice in levels

Worked

"Your package is held. Pay a $1.95 redelivery fee at this link in 24 hours." Score: urgency (24 hours), mismatch (link is not the carrier's domain), and an unusual payment ask. Three tells, rule fires. Verify by opening the carrier's own app; the package is fine. Verdict: scam.

Faded

"This is the IRS. You owe back taxes. Pay today with gift cards or face arrest." Score the tells: urgency ___, secret/irreversible payment ___, mismatch ___. Does the rule fire?

Worked solutionUrgency (today, arrest), irreversible payment (gift cards), and impersonation. The IRS never demands gift cards or threatens immediate arrest by phone. Rule fires: scam. Verify only through irs.gov, never the caller.

Independent

"Hi, it's your CEO. I'm in a meeting and need you to buy five $200 gift cards for client gifts and send the codes. Reply only here." Score it and give your verdict and verify step.

Model answerUrgency, secret/irreversible payment (gift-card codes), and channel-locking ("reply only here") to block verification. Scam. Verify by reaching the CEO on a known internal channel, not by replying.

Far transfer

Different surface, no urgency: someone you met on a dating app three weeks ago, never in person, now needs $3,000 wired for a medical emergency and promises to repay. Which tell do you rely on, and what is the call?

Model answer / rubricNo urgency tell, so you rely on the irreversible payment to someone never met in person. That single tell, plus the no-meeting fact, is enough: do not send. Pass if the answer drops the urgency rule and keys on the irreversible-payment-to-a-stranger tell.

Make it yours

Set up your real defenses today: turn on transaction alerts in your banking app, save your bank's real fraud number from the back of your card into your phone, and write the one sentence you will say to yourself when a message rushes you. Then tell one family member the trusted-channel rule, because scammers often target whoever in a household is least prepared.

Common trap

The wrong belief: "If they know my real details, like my name, address, and last four card digits, it must really be my bank." It is tempting because that information feels private and hard to get.

Diagnostic case: A caller recites your full name, address, and the last four digits, then asks for the one-time code. All of that is for sale in leaked-data lists for pennies; the code is the one thing they cannot buy. Repair move: judge the request, not the rapport. The moment anyone asks for a code, password, or irreversible payment, the known details become irrelevant and the rule fires.

Module check

Reason about the structure of the scam, not the cover story.

Why does the rule key on a request for a one-time code rather than on whether the caller knows your personal details?

  • Personal details leak cheaply and prove nothing, while the one-time code is the exact thing a scammer still needs to take over the account.
  • Personal details are impossible to obtain illegally.
  • One-time codes are never used by real banks.
  • Knowing details always proves identity.

Correct: the rule targets the decisive asset, the code, because everything else is cheap to fake or buy. The tempting wrong answer treats known details as proof, which is exactly the trap the scam exploits.

A message scores only one tell, a slightly-off sender domain, with no urgency or payment ask. What is the reasoned next step?

  • Do not act on the message, and verify on a trusted channel; one tell is enough to switch channels even if it is not enough to be certain.
  • Reply to the sender to ask if they are legitimate.
  • Click the link since there is no payment request.
  • Ignore the mismatch because urgency is absent.

Correct: verifying on a trusted channel is free and defeats a false positive harmlessly, so any tell justifies it. Replying to the sender uses the untrusted channel and is exactly what the scammer wants.

Why does switching to a trusted channel defeat almost every version of this, whatever the story?

  • The scammer controls only the channel they contacted you on; a real issue still exists on your bank's own app or number, so verifying there removes their script entirely.
  • Trusted channels are encrypted and texts are not.
  • Banks only ever use apps, never phone or text.
  • It makes the scammer's message disappear.

Correct: the scam depends on you staying inside the channel they own; a real institution is reachable independently and a fake one is not. The encryption answer is a distractor; the defense is about who controls the channel, not how it is secured.

A romance contact of three weeks, never met in person, asks for a $3,000 wire for an emergency. There is no time pressure. Which tell carries the decision?

  • Urgency, because all scams are urgent.
  • The irreversible payment to someone you have never met in person; the slow build removes urgency, so that tell carries it.
  • Mismatch, because the message is from an app.
  • None apply, so it is probably fine.

Correct: slow-trust scams drop the urgency tell on purpose, so the irreversible-payment-to-a-stranger tell becomes decisive. "All scams are urgent" is the exact assumption these cons are built to exploit.

Which request, by itself, should flag a message as a scam every time, regardless of how official it looks?

  • Asking you to log in to your account.
  • Asking you to pay with gift cards, or to read back a one-time code or password.
  • Telling you a payment posted.
  • Listing your recent transactions.

Correct: gift cards and shared codes/passwords are never part of a legitimate institution's process, so either is a standalone flag. "Asking you to log in" is what real and fake messages both say; the danger there is the link, which is why you go to the app yourself rather than treat the login ask alone as the tell.
