Inflation is the gradual rise in the general price level, which means each dollar
buys a little less over time. It is the reason cash sitting idle loses purchasing
power even though its dollar count never drops.

Inflation compounds like interest, in reverse: at 3% a year, prices roughly
double in about 24 years (rule of 72: `72 ÷ 3`), so $100 of groceries today costs
about $200 then. The real return on money is its growth rate minus inflation,
`real ≈ nominal − inflation`. A savings account paying 1% while inflation runs 3%
loses about 2% of purchasing power a year, which is why long-term money is usually
invested rather than held as cash.
