A tax-advantaged account (401(k), IRA, HSA) is a container the government lets you
invest in with a tax break, in exchange for rules about when you can take the
money out. The break is large because it removes the yearly drag of taxes on
gains, letting compounding work on the full balance.

Two main shapes. Traditional: contributions are pre-tax now, the balance grows
untaxed, and you pay income tax on withdrawals in retirement. Roth:
contributions are after-tax now, and qualified withdrawals are tax-free later. A
401(k) match is an employer contribution on top of yours, an immediate return of
50% or 100% on matched dollars before any market growth. The decision rule:
capture the full match first, because no investment reliably beats an instant
100% return.
