Compound interest is interest earned on both the original amount and on the
interest already added, so the balance grows on a curve rather than a straight
line. The same mechanism that builds wealth in an index fund builds a credit-card
balance you cannot escape.

Formally, an amount `P` growing at annual rate `r`, compounded `n` times a year
for `t` years, becomes `A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)`. For a once-a-year approximation,
`A ≈ P(1 + r)^t`. At 7% a year, money roughly doubles every ten years (the
"rule of 72": years to double ≈ 72 ÷ rate-percent). Time is the dominant input:
starting earlier beats contributing more, because the exponent is time.
